  • Cathy Campbell

    I arrived at The Washington Post in 1984 as a newly minted MBA with two years of experience at a small newspaper in a small city in North Carolina. I was among the first women to ever hold a management position in the circulation department at the Post. I was barely 26. As a woman with a college-degree, I clearly needed hazing. "We need you to experience what our independent distributors experience: Drive a truck! Find carriers! Deliver newspapers! Collect the money!"   • Mei-Mei Chan
    President and Publisher
    The News-Press Media Group

    When Garrison Keillor gave up Prairie Home Companion in 1987, I went in search of Lake Wobegon for USA Today. I drove through a number of small towns in Minnesota, bopping into bars and coffee shops, connecting with locals who knew for a fact they were the inspiration for his show, or who didn't know him at all. I was about seven months pregnant and I recall sitting in my rental car, saying farewell to a handful of townsfolk as I was headed out. One of them had been keeping his distance. Finally, he approached, leaned into the car, and said, "Ms. Chung. I have to just say, you do a great job. I'm a big fan." I paused, then said, "Thank you." Call me a wimp but I didn't have the heart to correct him. So I'm pretty sure there's a plaque at the local hotel that says, "Connie Chung stayed here." This memory has both a half full and half empty moral. Half full: Say what you will about Connie's journalistic skills, she broke all kinds of barriers, including into the backwoods towns of Minnesota, inspiring generations of young Asian journalists. On the half empty side: My daughter is 22. And, even today, to too many eyes, we still all look alike, and thus we're still often treated generically alike.

  • Dawn Garcia
    Deputy Director, Knight Fellowships
    Stanford University

    In the spring of 2006, the Journalism & Women Symposium Board of Directors met with a specific challenge: How could we afford all the good work we wanted to do for women in journalism? We learned our lesson: women in journalism CAN do it!

  • Dorothy Gilliam
    Senior Research Scientist and Director, Prime Movers Media,
    The George Washington University

    When I became the first black woman reporter at The Washington Post in October, 1961, I purposely did not reveal my misadventures in the segregated nation's Capitol to my editors or white reporters. I felt it would make it harder for another black female to be hired if I complained every time cabs whizzed past me near deadline, when a doorman on swanky Cathedral Avenue told me to go to the maid's entrance, when I couldn't be seated in a restaurant or shown an apartment in a predominantly white neighborhood. My only resort was to suck in the insults and suffer silently. Fast forward 20 years or so. In the early 1980s, a young intern from Harvard named Michel McQueen came to work for the summer, joining a growing number of black journalists at the newspaper. One day she came and complained to all who would listen that she had just been insulted. It seems she'd gone to The Wall Street Journal's Washington office for an interview and someone there assumed she was applying for a job as a secretary. Michel was irate. I smiled inwardly as she spoke and thanked God for the civil rights and women's rights movements. This young black woman -- now Michel Martin of National Public Radio -- felt free to complain! Her freedom helped to liberate me and that moment crystallized what black women of my generation had to overcome.

  • Jeanne Grinstead

  • Wanda Lloyd
    Executive Editor
    Montgomery (Ala.) Advertiser

    Right after I started my first full-time newsroom job, on the copy desk of the Providence Evening Bulletin, I was standing at the sink in the ladies room and a woman -- her job was to take dictation from reporters out in the field -- turned to me to ask a question. "Where did you come from?" she asked me. I was taken aback. She asked again, adding that the other ladies in the dictation pool were curious as to how someone like me had made it to the copy desk, which until then was a staff of men, all of them much older than I was as a recent college graduate. And there were fewer than a handful of other professional women in the newsroom at the time. I explained to her that I had studied (but not majored in) journalism in college and that I had been a Dow Jones Newspaper Fund intern in copy editing, and had attended the copy desk course at Temple University, that I had been editor of my high school and college newspapers. I reminded her that I had been a copy-editing intern the summer before in Providence, and at the end of the internship I was offered a full-time position to return the next year after graduation. I didn't realize it at that moment but now I know that at age 21, I was somewhat of a pioneer. Yet, then and later (perhaps even now) I haven't always known if the question refers to my gender or to my race as an African American. In the end, I guess it doesn't matter "where I came from," but that I have been here now for 40 years, and that I have worked to make it better for those who have come behind me.

  • Arlene Morgan
    Associate Dean
    Columbia University Graduate School of Journalism

    I think the most important time in my career as a leader in The Inquirer newsroom for women was when I led a women's caucus that came together during the mid-'80s to challenge the pay scale, promotion opportunities and assignments that women were getting compared to the men. We found that women in the same jobs were being paid significantly less than the men. For instance, I found that the man I followed in the role as deputy metro editor who was in charge of vetting applications and putting forth potential hires was earning about $6,000 more than I was. And this was after I had created a major recruiting database to replace a system that was dysfunctional at best. I also led the newsroom in looking for more diverse hires. We gathered -- at that point we were about 90 women strong -- and called a meeting with the top editors. At the time, Gene Roberts was the editor and I think he really did not quite get the depth of anger among the women in the newsroom until he came to our caucus meeting. To his credit, he created a committee to meet with him to hash out our demands. We told him that it did not matter if he thought a woman with a child had too many outside the newsroom responsibilities to go overseas. If she was qualified, it was up to her to make the decision if she should go or not; not his. To that point, only single women were assigned to foreign postings. We also said that it was time for a women city editor to be appointed. Within months, Fran Dauth was named the city editor and Jane Eisner, who had a six-month-old, was sent to the London Bureau. And many of us, including me, got promotions and salary adjustments. When I look back on that, I am so proud that we banded together, stood up for equality and won. From that point on, the newsroom was a different place and so much the better for it.

  • Donna M. Reed
    Vice President, Content
    Media General, Inc.

    The defining moment that set my career course came the day I was hired as a part-time sports correspondent at The Tampa Tribune. I was fresh out of graduate school with a thin portfolio of clips and the managing editor suggested I fatten my clip file to bolster my chances for a full-time news job. He dismissed me with the offhand suggestion I ask sport editor Tom McEwen if he had anything for me. The lesson then and now for journalists, particularly for women, is to take the road less traveled by seeking out assignments others slough off, or by volunteering for lousy beats, even if it means covering junior high school football games for months. Don't limit yourself early on or reach too high too soon. I meet young women today, fresh out of school, asking for prime anchor jobs before they've had the opportunity to cover weekend general assignment. I didn't apply that day in 1974 to be a vice president of news; I applied for any job that involved reporting. The sports correspondent's job led to an even less desirable remote bureau job that led to the less-than-glamorous night police beat. You get the picture.
